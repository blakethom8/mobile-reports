I. The Big Picture — Three Frameworks, One Project

Three entirely separate regulatory frameworks govern different aspects of this project. Understanding their independence — and where they intersect — is the foundation of the permitting strategy.

Framework Governs Authority Type
SB9 (State Law) Whether a new residential unit is allowed on this parcel per zoning Orinda Planning Dept Ministerial
Chapter 17.32 Exception Whether structures/grading are permitted within the creek setback Orinda Planning / Zoning Admin Discretionary
Building & Grading Permits Whether the construction is safe per building code; authorizes breaking ground Contra Costa County Building Dept Last in sequence
The building permit is what actually authorizes construction. SB9 is a zoning sign-off that unlocks the building permit. The 17.32 exception (if required) must also be resolved before the building permit is issued. CC County Building will not issue a building permit until every upstream agency — Orinda Planning, MOFD, CCCSD — has signed off. Nothing gets built without it.

III. The Grading Entanglement — Why Separation May Be Difficult

Earlier analysis suggested the house and back-area improvements could be cleanly separated for permitting purposes. That framing needs to be qualified given the realities of a hillside site.

The grading permit — issued by CC County Building, separate from but usually concurrent with the building permit — covers all earthwork on the parcel above threshold yardage. For a new home on a 10-acre hillside lot, the grading plan must be prepared by a licensed civil engineer and address:

  • All cut and fill volumes across the site
  • All retaining walls (location, height, materials, structural engineering)
  • How drainage from the entire disturbed area routes and exits the site
  • Post-development runoff vs. pre-development (CC County and C.3 requirements)

The complication for 38 Oakwood: some of the proposed retention walls run along the creek setback zone. If any portion of those walls or their footings, excavation, or associated drainage routing falls within the setback, the grading permit for the full site becomes entangled with the 17.32 exception requirement.

The Critical Engineering Question

Your civil engineer needs to determine — specifically and in writing — whether the grading plan for the house can be filed in two phases:

Phase 1: House pad, primary access, ADU, garage, retaining walls entirely outside the creek setback, stable and drainage-complete without back-area work.

Phase 2: Back-area retention walls, swale management, fire pit area, grading within or near the setback — filed after 17.32 resolves.

If Phase 1 is genuinely self-contained, Scenario D is achievable. If the Phase 1 drainage routing must pass through the setback zone, or if Phase 1 retaining walls are structurally interdependent with Phase 2 walls, separation is not feasible and you're in Scenario B or C.

IV. SB9 — What It Is and Why It Still Matters

Senate Bill 9 (effective January 2022) requires cities to grant ministerial approval for a new single-family home on a single-family parcel — without hearings, neighbor notification, or CEQA. Without SB9, a new home on this lot goes through Orinda's standard Design Review — a fully discretionary process where the City Engineer's objections carry real weight in public hearings.

SB9's value: It eliminates the Design Review pathway — no hearings, no CEQA, no neighbor objections on the house itself. Ryan can't object to the house in a public forum if SB9 governs. His leverage is limited to the drainage/engineering review component of the SB9 completeness review, which is narrower and more legally bounded than Design Review.

Why SB9 Is Separate from the Building Permit

SB9 = zoning approval (is the use allowed here?). Building permit = construction approval (is the building built safely?). These are governed by different codes, different departments, and different standards. SB9 does not authorize construction. It is the prerequisite that allows CC County to issue the building permit. The full sequence is:

Orinda Planning (SB9 sign-off) + MOFD approval + CCCSD approval → then CC County Building Department issues Building Permit → then construction may begin.

V. §17.4.6 — Creek Setback Rules (Verbatim)

§17.4.6.B — Channel Type Definitions:
Type I: A major channel receiving water from several tributaries (San Pablo Creek, Moraga Creek, Brookside Creek, Laterwasser Creek). Setback: 45 ft.
Type II: Ephemeral/intermittent or perennial tributary channel with well-defined channel bed and banks. Setback: 30 ft.
Type III: Ephemeral swale or small channel that does not have well-defined bed and banks. Setback: 15 ft.
Type IV: Man-made channel (road-side ditch) where no channel previously existed. Setback: 0 ft.
§17.4.6.C — Restrictions:
"No structure may be built... and no grading is permitted in the setback unless: (1) City Engineer approval (§16.64.220.C); and (2) Exception under Chapter 17.32. If a project is proposed on the bank or in an area that is undeveloped and in a natural state, the City may hire a biologist to perform a biotic assessment at the applicant's expense."
The type determination is the fulcrum. Ryan's push to call the back drainage Type II (30 ft setback) vs. Type III/IV (15 ft or 0 ft) doubles or eliminates the restricted zone. Fight this factual determination with an independent geomorphology expert before any permit is filed.

VI. Chapter 17.32 — Exception Permit: Full Findings Criteria

If any structure or grading falls within the creek setback, an Exception Permit under Chapter 17.32 is required before a building permit can be issued. The decision-maker must find ALL of the following (§17.32.4):

Finding A — Site Uniqueness or No Harm (Either/Or)

The strict application of zoning regulations will either: (1) deprive the property of rights enjoyed by others in the same zone due to the site's unique characteristics (size, shape, topography, or existing development limitations); OR (2) serve no land use planning purpose because there is NO negative impact whatsoever on the semi-rural character of Orinda or on the privacy, views, and well-being of neighboring properties.

Finding B — Compliance with Intent

The exception will result in development that substantially complies with the intent and purpose of the zoning requirements, including design review, of the land use district.

Finding C — For Existing Structures Only

(1) The exception does not substantially increase an existing nonconformity; AND (2) the exception is necessary to preserve or enhance an architecturally or functionally appropriate feature of the existing structure.

§17.32.5 — Conditions: "An exception shall be subject to conditions to assure it does not constitute a grant of special privileges inconsistent with other properties in the same zone."

Best argument for back-area improvements (Finding A, path 2): On a 10-acre hillside lot, retention walls and drainage management in the back area have zero negative impact on neighbors, privacy, views, or the semi-rural character of Orinda. The concrete v-ditch is man-made infrastructure — managing drainage into it is maintenance, not new development. Proactively retain your own biologist to document no sensitive habitat.

X. Strategic Recommendations

  1. Get a definitive answer from your civil engineer on phased grading feasibility. This is the single most important next step. Ask explicitly: can Phase 1 (house pad, ADU, garage, primary access) be designed as stable and drainage-complete without back-area work? Their answer determines which scenario you're in.
  2. Lock in the creek setback type before filing anything. Commission an independent hydrology/geomorphology report. Type II (30 ft) vs. Type III (15 ft) or Type IV (0 ft) is a factual question resolvable with evidence. Do this before the setback determination is formally issued.
  3. Retain your own biologist proactively. Document the back drainage area as an ephemeral swale or man-made drainage system before the City can require their own biotic assessment under adversarial circumstances. Control the characterization.
  4. File Chapter 17.32 as early as possible — start the clock. Even if phased grading is achievable, the 17.32 process takes 6–15 months regardless. Start it now so it runs concurrently with design and the house permitting track.
  5. Submit SB9 with a narrowly scoped drainage plan. Have Fanny draft a cover letter asserting SB9's ministerial standard limits the City to objective standards only — whole-parcel drainage analysis is not an objective standard explicitly listed in the SB9 application form.
  6. Get Ryan's swale connection demand in writing with specific code authority. If he can't cite a specific objective standard, it's not enforceable in a ministerial SB9 review.
  7. Document everything. Every request, every verbal condition, every completeness comment. A writ of mandate proceeding (requiring the City to act on a ministerial application) is a real tool if SB9 is wrongly held incomplete for discretionary reasons.
