I. Background: The Regulatory Landscape

This property sits on a 10-acre hillside lot in the Orinda hills with a drainage area in the back that runs toward a concrete v-ditch at the rear of the property. The City Engineer (Ryan O'Kane) has been pushing to designate the back drainage as a wetland and has additionally required that natural back-area swales be connected to the concrete v-ditch as a condition of any work in that zone.

The permitting picture is complicated because three entirely separate regulatory frameworks govern different aspects of the project — and they interact in non-obvious ways:

Framework Governs Authority Type
SB9 (State Law) Whether a new residential unit is allowed on this parcel Orinda Planning Dept Ministerial
Chapter 17.32 Exception Whether structures/grading are permitted in the creek setback Orinda Planning / Zoning Admin Discretionary
Building & Grading Permits Whether the building is constructed safely per building code Contra Costa County Building Dept Last in sequence

Understanding this separation is the foundation of the entire strategy. SB9 and Chapter 17.32 are not the same process. The building permit is not the same as either. Each has its own sequence, timeline, and decision-maker — and the key strategic move is keeping them as decoupled as possible.

II. SB9 — What It Is and Why It Matters for This Project

What SB9 Does

Senate Bill 9 (effective January 2022) requires cities to grant ministerial approval for the construction of a new single-family home or second unit on a single-family parcel — without discretionary review. The word "ministerial" is critical: it means the City checks only objective, yes/no standards. No hearings. No neighbor notification. No CEQA. No Design Review.

Why SB9 matters here: Without SB9, a new home on this lot would require Orinda's standard Design Review — a discretionary process with public hearings, neighbor notification, CEQA analysis, and Planning Commission or Zoning Administrator judgment calls. That's the process where an adversarial City Engineer can inject objections through every channel. SB9 eliminates that exposure entirely for the house itself.

What SB9 Does NOT Cover

SB9 approves only the housing unit and its compliance with objective zoning standards (setbacks from property lines, height, FAR). It does not cover or replace:

  • Creek setback exceptions (Chapter 17.32 / §17.30.5)
  • Grading permits for back-area improvements
  • Retention walls, fire pits, or landscaping in the creek setback
  • Tree removal permits
  • State/federal environmental permits (§1602, §404)

The Eligibility Checklist

To qualify for SB9, all items on Orinda's eligibility checklist must be "Yes" or "N/A." Most are straightforward for this property. However, Item 12 contains a material risk:

Item 12 — Eligibility Exclusions: The property must be outside any of the following: Prime farmland, Wetlands as defined under federal law, Hazardous waste sites, Protected species habitat, Conservation easements, Lands identified for conservation in any adopted plan, Historic landmarks or districts.

If Ryan O'Kane successfully obtains a wetland designation on the back drainage area, it could cause Item 12 to fail — potentially blocking SB9 ministerial approval entirely and forcing the project into full discretionary Design Review. This is the highest-stakes outcome of the wetland fight.

Why SB9 Is Separate from the Building Permit

These are two entirely different regulatory frameworks:

  • SB9/Zoning: Governed by Title 17 of the Orinda Municipal Code (land use). Asks: Is this use allowed here? Does it meet zoning standards? Issued by Orinda Planning Dept.
  • Building Permit: Governed by the California Building Code. Asks: Is this building constructed safely? Issued by Contra Costa County Building Dept (Orinda contracts them). Requires all other agency approvals first before CC County will process.

The sequence is: Orinda Planning (SB9/zoning sign-off) → MOFD → CCCSD → then CC County Building. SB9 approval is a prerequisite for the building permit, not a substitute for it.

III. §17.4.6 — Creek Setback Rules (Verbatim)

The applicable zoning code provision governing creek setbacks was retrieved directly from Orinda's Municipal Code (Municode, version Sep 8, 2025):

§17.4.6.B — Channel Type Definitions:
Type I: A major channel that receives water from several tributaries (San Pablo Creek, Moraga Creek, Brookside Creek, Laterwasser Creek).
Type II: An ephemeral/intermittent or perennial tributary channel that has well-defined channel bed and banks.
Type III: An ephemeral swale or other small channel distinguished from Type I and II because it does not have well-defined channel bed and banks.
Type IV: A man-made channel, such as a road-side ditch, that occurs where no channel previously existed.
§17.4.6.C — Restrictions:
"No structure may be built, except for second story additions that do not encroach into the required setback beyond the existing first floor structure, and no grading is permitted in the setback of a water channel, unless the project meets the following conditions:
1. The project proponent obtains approval from the City Engineer; and
2. The project proponent is granted an exception under Chapter 17.32.
If a project is proposed on the bank of a water channel or in an area of the site that is undeveloped and in a natural state, then the City may hire a biologist to perform a biotic assessment at the applicant's expense and the decision-maker shall consider the findings of this biotic assessment when reviewing the exception application."
Channel type is the fulcrum of the entire fight. Type II (well-defined bed and banks) carries a 30-foot setback. Type III (ephemeral swale, no well-defined bed/banks) carries a 15-foot setback. Type IV (man-made ditch) carries 0 feet. Ryan O'Kane's push to classify the back drainage as Type II — or worse, as a wetland — is an attempt to double the setback and dramatically constrain what can be built or graded in the back of the property.

The Swale Connection Demand

Ryan has indicated that natural back-area swales need to be connected to the existing concrete v-ditch at the rear of the property. This is a City Engineer drainage condition — not a zoning standard and not an SB9 requirement. However, executing this connection would likely require grading within the creek setback zone, which triggers both:

  • City Engineer approval under §16.64.220.C (Ryan's approval)
  • Chapter 17.32 Exception Permit (discretionary — see Section IV)

This creates a circular trap: Ryan demands swale connection → swale connection requires his own approval → he can withhold approval or impose conditions. This is the core conflict that must be resolved strategically, not administratively.

IV. Chapter 17.32 — Exception Permit: Full Findings Criteria

Any structure or grading within the creek setback requires an Exception Permit under Chapter 17.32, in addition to City Engineer approval. This permit is discretionary — meaning a human decision-maker weighs the evidence and makes a judgment call. The exact findings required (§17.32.4), retrieved directly from Municode, are:

§17.32.4 — Standards (All Must Be Satisfied)

Finding A — Site Uniqueness or No Harm (Either/Or)

The strict application of zoning regulations will either:

(1) Deprive the subject property of rights enjoyed by other properties in the vicinity within the same zone, because of the site's unique characteristics (irregular or constrained size, shape, topography, or limitations from existing development) that limit design options; OR

(2) Serve no land use planning purpose because there is NO negative impact whatsoever on the semi-rural character of Orinda or on the privacy, views, and general well-being of neighboring properties, as a result of the size, shape, location or topography of the site.

Finding B — Compliance with Intent

The exception will result in development that substantially complies with the intent and purpose of the zoning requirements, including design review, of the land use district.

Finding C — Existing Structures Only

(1) The exception does not substantially increase the degree of an existing nonconformity; AND
(2) The exception is necessary to preserve or enhance an architecturally or functionally appropriate feature of the existing structure.

Note: Finding C is applicable only for projects affecting an existing structure — likely not primary here since this is new construction.

§17.32.5 — Conditions: "An exception shall be subject to such conditions as will assure that the adjustment does not constitute a grant of special privileges inconsistent with the limitations on other properties in the vicinity and zone."

Best Argument Path for Back-Area Improvements

Finding A(2) is the strongest angle for retention walls and drainage work on a 10-acre hillside lot with no visible impact on neighbors:

  • The property is 10 acres — back-area drainage improvements are remote from any neighboring structure
  • Retention walls and swale management are standard hillside maintenance, not unusual development
  • No impact whatsoever on semi-rural character, privacy, views, or well-being of adjacent properties
  • The concrete v-ditch itself is man-made infrastructure — managing drainage into it is drainage maintenance, not habitat disturbance
Biotic Assessment Risk: §17.4.6.C explicitly allows the City to require a biotic assessment when work is proposed in an undeveloped/natural area adjacent to a watercourse. If the back area is characterized as undeveloped and natural (which Ryan will argue), the City can hire a biologist at applicant's expense. Seasonal survey requirements for certain species (nesting birds, amphibians) can add 6+ months to the timeline if the window is missed.

Process and Timing

  • §17.32.3: The exception permit must be obtained before the building permit.
  • Projects under 1,500 sf of new floor area → Zoning Administrator (faster)
  • Projects 1,500 sf or more → Planning Commission
  • For back-area site improvements (not a new building), likely Zoning Administrator jurisdiction
Scenario Estimated Timeline
Best case — no biotic assessment, cooperative staff 4–6 months
Realistic — completeness rounds, standard CEQA categorical exemption 6–9 months
Adversarial — biotic assessment required, multiple completeness resets 9–15 months
Denial + appeal to Planning Commission 12–24+ months

V. The Separation Strategy — SB9 vs. Chapter 17.32

The Core Idea

The house footprint is outside the creek setback. The back-area improvements (retention walls, fire pit, grading, swale work) are what would fall inside or near the setback. These are different projects serving different purposes and can potentially be permitted on separate tracks.

If successfully separated:
SB9 for the house = ministerial approval in 30–60 days once deemed complete.
Chapter 17.32 for back-area = 6–15 months on a parallel track.

You break ground on the house while the back-area fight runs in the background. By the time the house is framed, the exception process may be resolved. If it isn't, you can obtain a Certificate of Occupancy and live in the house while back-area improvements continue. The creek isn't going anywhere.
If bundled together:
The 17.32 discretionary exception process must resolve before SB9 can proceed, because Orinda's own SB9 page states: "If your project requires a discretionary permit, you must obtain approval for all discretionary permits BEFORE applying for an SB 9 Unit application." A 12-month 17.32 fight delays breaking ground on everything by 12 months.

The Drainage Plan Complication

The SB9 submittal requirements include a Drainage/Improvement Plan and a Drainage Report. This was not anticipated in earlier analysis and creates a potential entanglement point. The drainage report must include:

  • Calculations showing post-development runoff does not exceed pre-development runoff
  • How stormwater is conveyed through/onto the subdivision
  • C.3 stormwater treatment compliance

The question is: does "runoff flowing onto/through the subdivision" require the drainage plan to address the back-area swales, or only the impervious surfaces being created by the house?

Interpretation Who Benefits Argument
Narrow: Drainage plan covers only new impervious surfaces (house, driveway, ADU, garage). Back-area drainage is existing natural state, unchanged. Applicant SB9 is ministerial — scope limited to proposed development. C.3 compliance calculated for new impervious area only. Natural back drainage unchanged, no permit needed for status quo.
Broad: Drainage plan must show whole-parcel hydrology including back-area routing. City Engineer review encompasses swale-to-ditch connection. Ryan / City Runoff "flowing through the subdivision" includes all drainage pathways. Can't isolate new impervious area from existing drainage network on a 10-acre hillside.

Key Legal Argument to Preserve

SB9's ministerial character limits what the City can demand as a condition of completeness. Under Government Code §65913.4, a ministerial application may only be denied or conditioned based on objective standards — not discretionary judgments. If Ryan's interpretation of the drainage plan scope goes beyond objective standards explicitly required by the SB9 application form, it is legally vulnerable. This is the argument Fanny should be prepared to make if Ryan attempts to hold the SB9 application incomplete on drainage grounds.

VI. Ryan O'Kane's Leverage Points — Mapped and Analyzed

Ryan O'Kane operates as Orinda's City Engineer. His authority surfaces at multiple points in the permitting process. Understanding precisely where he has power — and where he doesn't — is essential for strategy.

If imposed, ask for it in writing with the specific code authority. Challenge whether it applies to private drainage on a private 10-acre lot.

VII. Scenarios — How This Could Play Out

🟢 Scenario 1 — Best Case: Clean Separation, Type III/IV Ruling

Ryan accepts (or is overruled in) the creek type determination as Type III swale (15 ft setback) or the back v-ditch as Type IV (0 ft). House footprint is outside the 15-ft setback. Back-area improvements stay outside the setback or are scoped to avoid it. SB9 drainage plan is narrowly scoped to new impervious surfaces. Ryan's Engineering Review has nothing material to object to.

Timeline: SB9 approved in ~60 days. Building permit in ~6 months. House under construction within 6–8 months of application.

Probability: Possible but requires proactive effort — lock in Type III/IV determination with independent expert support before any application is filed.

🟡 Scenario 2 — Middle Ground: SB9 Proceeds, 17.32 Fights in Background

Ryan contests the channel type (pushes Type II), but wetland designation fails or is successfully challenged. House footprint is outside the setback regardless of Type II vs. III ruling. SB9 proceeds for the house. Chapter 17.32 exception for back-area improvements (retention walls, grading) proceeds as a parallel discretionary process. Drainage plan scoped narrowly for SB9 purposes.

Timeline: SB9 in 60–120 days. House permit in 6–8 months. Back-area work resolved in 9–15 months (during construction or after move-in).

Probability: Most likely outcome if the separation strategy is successfully defended. The key fight is holding the SB9 scope narrow in the face of Ryan's drainage expansion attempt.

🟠 Scenario 3 — Delayed: Drainage Entanglement Forces Bundling

Ryan successfully argues the SB9 drainage report must cover whole-parcel hydrology, including back-area swales. City holds SB9 application incomplete pending resolution of swale-to-ditch connection. The 17.32 exception process must begin before SB9 can proceed. Everything is sequential — house is on hold while exception process runs.

Timeline: Everything delayed 9–15 months. Significant cost impact given construction cost escalation and carrying costs on land.

Response: Invoke SB9 ministerial limits aggressively. Fanny sends formal letter asserting the City cannot impose discretionary conditions on a ministerial SB9 review. Consider filing a writ of mandate if City refuses to act.

🔴 Scenario 4 — Worst Case: Wetland Designation Succeeds

Ryan obtains a wetland designation on the back drainage area — whether through CDFW, USACE, or City's own characterization. Item 12 of the SB9 eligibility checklist fails. SB9 is denied. Project is forced into full discretionary Design Review — public hearings, CEQA analysis, neighbor notification. The full weight of Ryan's opposition is now channeled into a discretionary process where it has maximum effect.

Timeline: Unknown — 18-36+ months to navigate Design Review and potential appeals.

Response: Challenge the wetland designation through formal USACE Jurisdictional Determination. The Army Corps — not the City Engineer — has final authority on federal wetland designations. This is a separate process and can be pursued aggressively in parallel.

X. Strategic Recommendations

  1. Lock in the Creek Setback Determination before filing anything. Get an independent hydrology/geomorphology expert to assess the channel type. Document clearly that the back drainage area is a Type III swale (no well-defined bed and banks) or that the v-ditch is Type IV (man-made). This is the factual foundation every other decision rests on.
  2. Consider a proactive biotic assessment — on your terms. If you hire your own certified biologist now, before the City requires one, you control the framing of what the back area contains. A biologist who documents that the back drainage is an ephemeral swale with no sensitive species is a far better outcome than one the City hires under adversarial circumstances.
  3. Fight the wetland designation through USACE, not through Ryan. A formal Jurisdictional Determination request to the Army Corps of Engineers is the right venue. The City Engineer cannot define federal wetlands unilaterally — that is Army Corps authority. A USACE JD finding of "no wetlands" is a powerful counter to Item 12 concerns.
  4. Submit SB9 with a narrowly scoped drainage plan. Drainage plan covers new impervious surfaces only. Back-area drainage is shown as existing, unchanged, natural state. Have Fanny draft a cover letter asserting that SB9's ministerial standard precludes the City from requiring whole-parcel drainage analysis as a condition of completeness.
  5. File Chapter 17.32 for back-area improvements in parallel, not sequentially. Start the exception process as soon as possible — it's long regardless. Running it during SB9 review and building permit processing minimizes net timeline impact. Retention walls and back-area grading can potentially be built after occupancy if the exception is still pending.
  6. Get Ryan's swale connection demand in writing with the specific code authority cited. If he can't cite a specific objective standard that requires it, the demand is not legally enforceable in the ministerial SB9 process.
  7. Document everything. Every email, every verbal communication, every demand. This project has the hallmarks of a situation where a writ of mandate (mandamus) proceeding against the City may eventually be necessary. An airtight paper trail is critical.
