2. Strategic Analysis of Ryan's Classifications

The Core Local Fight: Type II vs. Type III

Ryan has classified the primary eastern channel as Type II (30-ft setback). Your best local-level argument is that it should be Type III (15-ft setback). The distinction under Orinda Municipal Code §17.4.6 comes down to one question:

Does the channel have well-defined channel bed and banks?
Type II = YES → 30 ft setback
Type III = NO → 15 ft setback

If the feature is a broad, shallow drainage valley without clearly cut banks — a swale that disperses rather than concentrates flow — it is Type III. Cutting the setback from 30 ft to 15 ft meaningfully changes the buildable envelope.

The "Top of Bank" Measurement Is Approximate and Contestable

Ryan labeled both top-of-bank points as "APPROXIMATE." Under §17.4.6, setbacks are measured from:

"the top of the bank or from the furthest extent of banks undercut by creek, whichever results in the greatest setback."

A licensed surveyor and geotechnical engineer establishing the true top-of-bank with survey precision could move that line — and with it, the entire setback boundary.

The Blue Shaded Area — The Wetland Target Zone

The blue hatched polygon east-southeast of the building is almost certainly the area Ryan is flagging as a wetland or active ponding zone. This specific location — roughly where the swales converge near the California Buckeye tree — is the site of his state agency referral (Fish & Wildlife and Water Board). This is the precise area that needs soil sampling and a vegetation survey.

SECTION B — Wetland Classification: Laws, Definitions & How to Challenge

Key Finding: Orinda's Municipal Code does not define or regulate "wetlands" anywhere. If Ryan O'Kane is pursuing a wetland designation, he is operating under state and/or federal authority — not local city code. This is a fundamentally different and more serious regulatory track.

3. The Three-Factor Wetland Test — All Three Must Be Present

Every wetland framework — federal and California state — requires three factors to be simultaneously present to designate an area as a wetland. All three must be met. If any one factor fails, it is not a wetland by legal definition.

# Factor What It Requires Your Position at 38 Oakwood
1 Wetland Hydrology Soil saturated continuously or recurrently at or near the surface — typically 14+ consecutive days during the growing season Arguable Rain-only surface flow that drains within days likely fails this standard. Needs hydrological documentation.
2 Hydric Soils Soils with physical signs of prolonged anaerobic conditions: gray/blue-gray coloring (gleying), rust mottling, sulfurous odor, organic layers Potentially Strong Upland Orinda soils on a hillside slope are unlikely to be hydric. Soil samples are the most defensible test.
3 Hydrophytic Vegetation Dominant plants must be wetland-adapted species (OBL, FACW, or FAC on the NWPL) Strong The dominant tree is California Buckeye — not listed on the wetland plant list, presumed UPL (Upland). See Section 5.

4. The Governing Laws & Exact Statutory Citations

Federal Framework

Clean Water Act § 404 — 33 U.S.C. § 1344
Source: US EPA — https://www.epa.gov/cwa-404/clean-water-laws-regulations-and-executive-orders-related-section-404

Establishes the federal program regulating discharge of dredged or fill material into "waters of the United States, including wetlands." Administered by the US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE).

33 CFR Part 328.3 — Federal Definition of "Wetlands"
Source: Electronic Code of Federal Regulations — https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-33/chapter-II/part-328

"The term 'wetlands' means those areas that are inundated or saturated by surface or ground water at a frequency and duration sufficient to support, and that under normal circumstances do support, a prevalence of vegetation typically adapted for life in saturated soil conditions. Wetlands generally include swamps, marshes, bogs, and similar areas."

33 CFR Part 328.3(b)(3) — Federal Exclusion for Drainage Ditches
Source: 33 CFR Part 328 — https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-33/chapter-II/part-328

"Ditches (including roadside ditches) excavated wholly in and draining only dry land and that do not carry a relatively permanent flow of water" are expressly NOT waters of the United States.

Favorable The concrete v-ditches may qualify for this federal exemption.

USACE 1987 Wetland Delineation Manual — The Field Standard
Source: https://www.nan.usace.army.mil/Portals/37/docs/regulatory/Formdoc/wlman87.pdf

The operative field manual used by delineators to evaluate all three factors. Requires all three to be present simultaneously.

California State Framework

California Water Code § 13050(e) — "Waters of the State"
Source: California Porter-Cologne Water Quality Control Act (1969)

"'Waters of the state' means any surface water or groundwater, including saline waters, within the boundaries of the state."

Important This statute does not mention "wetlands." The SWRCB's claim of jurisdiction over wetlands as "waters of the state" has never been affirmed by a California court and is legally contested.

SWRCB Resolution No. 2019-0015 — California State Wetland Definition
Adopted April 2, 2019 | Effective May 28, 2020 | Source: https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/cwa401/wrapp.html

"An area is wetland if, under normal circumstances:
(1) the area has continuous or recurrent saturation of the upper substrate caused by groundwater, or shallow surface water, or both;
(2) the duration of such saturation is sufficient to cause anaerobic conditions in the upper substrate; and
(3) the area's vegetation is dominated by hydrophytes or the area lacks vegetation."

Burden of proof (verbatim from the Procedures):

"Where a water feature meets the Procedures' definition of a wetland, the burden is on the applicant to demonstrate that the wetland is not a water of the state."

Warning The burden falls on you once a wetland claim is made. This is why proactive soil sampling and vegetation surveys are critical before Ryan formalizes his position with a state agency.

California Fish & Game Code § 1602 — Streambed Alteration Agreement
Administered by CDFW — Source: https://biologistshandbook.com/permits/state-permits/section-1602-lake-and-streambed-alteration-agreement/

"'Any river, stream, or lake' includes those that are episodic (dry for periods of time) as well as those that are perennial. This includes ephemeral streams, desert washes, and watercourses with a subsurface flow."

Requires CDFW notification before any construction that alters the bed, banks, or flow of a waterway — even ephemeral ones. "Rain-only drainage" does not automatically exempt a feature from §1602 review.

5. Key Court Cases — Your Legal Ammunition

Two major recent decisions significantly weaken Ryan's position — one federal, one California state.

Case 1: Sackett v. EPA, 598 U.S. ___ (2023) — US Supreme Court

Held: CWA jurisdiction over wetlands requires a "continuous surface connection" to a traditionally navigable water — not just adjacency or hydrologic connectivity. This was a landmark narrowing of federal wetland jurisdiction.

Implication for 38 Oakwood: Even if the back drainage area has wetland characteristics, it may fall outside federal CWA jurisdiction if there is no continuous, unbroken surface connection to San Pablo Creek (a navigable water). Seasonal or intermittent connections are likely insufficient post-Sackett.

Source: Supreme Court of the United States, May 25, 2023

Case 2: San Joaquin Tributaries Authority v. SWRCB — Sacramento County Superior Court (2020)

Case No. 34-2019-80003133 | Decided December 17, 2020

Held: The court substantially limited the 2019 California Wetland Procedures, restricting them to waters regulated under the federal Clean Water Act and state ocean waters. The court found the SWRCB exceeded its authority under Water Code § 13170 in attempting to apply the Procedures to non-federal waters.

Implication for 38 Oakwood: If the drainage area is not jurisdictional under the federal CWA (likely post-Sackett), the 2019 California Wetland Procedures may not apply either. Ryan's ability to impose a state wetland designation may be significantly constrained.

Source: Downey Brand LLP Legal Alert — https://www.downeybrand.com/legal-alerts/judge-suspends-state-procedures...

Additional Legal Challenge — Waters of the State (2025)

A March 2025 analysis by Briscoe Prows Kao Ivester & Bazel LLP argues:

"Contrary to popular supposition, 'waters of the state' properly do not include 'wetlands.' The California Legislature had no intention of reaching wetlands when it enacted the statute in 1969... Their claim has never been examined or sanctioned by any court. It remains, in that sense, an open legal question."

Source: https://briscoefirm.com/are-wetlands-really-waters-of-the-state/ (March 2025)

Why it grows near drainage Buckeye grows along streamsides in the Bay Area by accessing water via deep roots — not because it tolerates saturated or anaerobic soils. This is a critical ecological distinction. USDA Plant Guide (AECA)

The "streamside buckeye" counterargument: Ryan or a state agency may argue that the buckeye's presence along the drainage indicates a riparian/wetland environment. The correct response: buckeyes are common along streambanks in the Bay Area and Coast Ranges precisely because they use deep roots to access subsurface water during summer drought — a drought-avoidance strategy, not a wetland adaptation. This is the ecological opposite of a hydrophytic species. A licensed botanist can confirm this in a vegetation survey.
