38 Oakwood Road — Creek Setback Strategy

Property: 38 Oakwood Road, Orinda, CA  |  APN: 269-190-006-B

Prepared: March 12, 2026

Subject: Creek Classification Conflict, Permitting Pathways, and Approval Strategy

Based on: Ryan O'Kane (City Engineer) site visit 2/25/2026 and setback determination 3/12/2026

Executive Summary

The City of Orinda issued a creek setback determination on March 12, 2026 classifying three water channels on the property as Type II (30-foot setback each) and one as Type III (15-foot setback). Following review of Ryan O'Kane's determination letter and the attached setback plan, several important findings emerge:

  • The proposed main house sits entirely outside all creek setbacks — no exception is required for the building itself.
  • Ryan's classification of channels as Type II contains a self-contradicting requirement that undermines the designation.
  • The City Engineer may have authority to approve required drainage work without a full Chapter 17.32 exception.
  • A two-track permitting strategy — SB-9 for the building, separate drainage track — is viable and recommended.

1. Ryan's Classification Conflict

Ryan O'Kane's determination letter simultaneously asserts two things that contradict each other:

The Contradiction

Assertion A: The channels are classified as Type II — meaning they have "well-defined channel bed and banks" per Orinda Municipal Code §17.4.6(B)(2).

Assertion B: The applicant must "reestablish the Type II water channel from the defined banks of the Type II channels to the concrete ditch."

Why these conflict: If the channel had well-defined bed and banks (as required for Type II status), no reestablishment would be necessary. The channel already exists. The act of requiring reestablishment implies the channel lacks defined features in that section — which is the defining characteristic of a Type III channel, not Type II.

"Type II channel is an ephemeral/intermittent or perennial tributary channel that has well-defined channel bed and banks."

"Type III channel is an ephemeral swale or other small channel that is distinguished from Type I and II channels because it does not have well-defined channel bed and banks."

— Orinda Municipal Code §17.4.6(B)(2–3)

The section Ryan is requiring to be reestablished — the transition zone between the natural channels and the existing concrete valley gutters — by definition does not have well-defined banks. That section is more accurately characterized as Type III (15-foot setback) or possibly even an informal flow path requiring no classification.

What This Means

  • The Type II classification may be challengeable for the transition segment specifically.
  • A licensed geomorphologist can assess whether the channels truly meet the "well-defined bed and banks" standard at each point along their length.
  • Any reclassification of even one segment from Type II → Type III reduces that setback from 30 feet to 15 feet — potentially recovering significant buildable/usable area.
  • Ryan's own reestablishment requirement is the strongest evidence supporting a Type III argument for the transition zone.
Recommended Action: Engage a licensed geomorphologist to conduct an independent channel assessment prior to submitting any permit applications. The goal is to determine whether any channel segments lack "well-defined bed and banks" and qualify for Type III reclassification. Submit findings to the City Engineer for reconsideration before proceeding with the 17.32 process.

2. Can the City Engineer Bypass the 17.32 Process?

Under Orinda Municipal Code §17.4.6(C), grading in a creek setback requires two approvals:

"No grading is permitted in the setback of a water channel, unless the project meets the following conditions: (1) The project proponent obtains approval from the City Engineer; and (2) The project proponent is granted an exception under Chapter 17.32." — Orinda Municipal Code §17.4.6(C)

Both conditions are listed as required. However, the drainage reestablishment work creates a unique situation that warrants a direct conversation with Ryan about whether a full Chapter 17.32 exception is truly necessary.

The Argument for City Engineer-Only Approval

Ryan's Catch-22

Ryan is simultaneously:

  • The City Engineer whose approval is required under §17.4.6(C)(1)
  • The official requiring the drainage reestablishment work
  • Gating that work behind a 17.32 exception — a process he also influences

The City is mandating work in the setback. It cannot simultaneously require that work AND demand a full discretionary exception to perform it without creating a procedural deadlock. This is a reasonable basis to request that Ryan exercise his City Engineer authority to approve the drainage work directly — without routing it through a public hearing process.

Key Question to Bring to Ryan

Ask directly: "You are requiring us to reestablish the Type II channel connection. That work requires grading in the setback. As City Engineer, can you approve this drainage work under your authority per §17.4.6(C)(1), treating it as an engineering condition of the project, without requiring a separate Chapter 17.32 exception application?"

Possible Outcomes

Ryan's Response Implication Path Forward
✅ Yes — approves drainage under engineering authority No 17.32 needed for drainage work SB-9 building track + City Engineer-approved drainage plan
⚠️ Partial — approves minor drainage, requires 17.32 for anything more 17.32 needed for expanded scope only Keep 17.32 narrow; scope it to drainage only, not building

3. SB-9 vs. Chapter 17.32 — The Two Permitting Paths

Why the Distinction Matters

These two processes are fundamentally different in nature, timeline, and risk:

Factor SB-9 (Ministerial) Chapter 17.32 (Discretionary)
Approval type By-right — city must approve if objective standards are met Judgment call — decision-maker can approve or deny
Public hearing None Required — Planning Commission
CEQA Exempt (ministerial action) Triggered — likely Mitigated Negative Declaration
Neighbor appeals Not applicable Decision can be appealed to City Council
Timeline 4–7 months to building permit 9–15 months (longer if appealed or CEQA required)
Creek setbacks Objective standards still apply — cannot encroach Exception allows encroachment if findings are made
Applies to this project Yes — building is outside setback Only if drainage work requires it

Chapter 17.32 Exception Standards

If a 17.32 exception is needed, the decision-maker must make the following findings per §17.32.4:

  • Finding A: Strict application of the setback either (1) deprives the property of rights enjoyed by similar nearby properties due to unique site characteristics (topography, shape, existing development), OR (2) serves no land use planning purpose because there is no negative impact on Orinda's semi-rural character or neighboring properties.
  • Finding B: The exception results in development that substantially complies with the intent of the zoning district.
Favorable Position: A 10-acre hillside property with three converging ephemeral drainages represents a genuinely unique topographic constraint not shared by typical flat or gently rolling RH-zoned properties nearby. Finding A(1) is a strong argument. Finding B is straightforward — a large estate on 10 acres is entirely consistent with Orinda's semi-rural character.

Key Risks of the 17.32 Path

Risk 1 — City Ties the Applications Together: If Orinda's Planning Department treats the building and drainage work as one integrated project, they may condition the building permit on 17.32 resolution — eliminating the parallel-track benefit. Clarify this explicitly at the SB-9 pre-application meeting.
Risk 2 — Appeal: Any neighbor can appeal a discretionary 17.32 decision to City Council, adding 2–4 months and uncertainty. Because the building itself is outside the setback, the appeal exposure is limited to the drainage scope — but it is a real risk to plan for.
Risk 3 — CEQA Triggers MND: The 17.32 exception is a discretionary action that triggers CEQA. A Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND) requires drafting, a 30-day public review period, and city adoption — adding 4–8 months to the 17.32 timeline. Scope the drainage work as narrowly as possible to minimize CEQA exposure.

4. The Building Is Outside the Setback — Leverage This

Based on the site plan and setback determination, the proposed main house sits entirely outside all creek setbacks — even under Ryan's Type II classification with 30-foot setbacks. This is the most important fact in this analysis.

What "Outside the Setback" Means Practically

  • No 17.32 exception required for the building. The structure does not encroach into any setback zone.
  • SB-9 ministerial approval is available for the building — no hearing, no CEQA, city cannot add discretionary conditions.
  • Construction can begin under SB-9 before the drainage permitting is fully resolved — because the building and drainage work are spatially separated on the site.
  • The drainage work zone and building footprint do not overlap. The channel reestablishment area is uphill/northeast (near the Buckeye tree convergence), while the house is downhill. These are independent scopes.

5. Landscaping in the Setback Area

Orinda Municipal Code §17.4.6(C) explicitly prohibits only structures and grading in the creek setback. Landscaping and vegetation are not addressed — and therefore not prohibited.

  • Native and naturalistic landscaping in the setback zones is permissible under the current code.
  • Planting native riparian species (willows, sedges, native grasses) in the setback area actively demonstrates environmental stewardship and can strengthen the project's position in any 17.32 or CEQA review.
  • The area near the Buckeye tree convergence is particularly appropriate for riparian enhancement plantings.
  • Confirm any specific planting plan with the City and CDFW as part of the drainage plan — they may have preferences but are unlikely to object to native vegetation establishment.
